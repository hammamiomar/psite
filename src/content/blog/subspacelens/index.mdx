## The Question

Last month I built a [real-time music visualizer](/blog/letting-computers-dance) that steers SDXL-Turbo at 50 FPS by clamping Sparse Autoencoder features — 20,480 dictionary atoms, each supposedly one visual concept. It worked. But the whole time I had this nagging feeling: *is this actually how the model organizes information?*

SAEs assume activations are **sparse** — each input decomposes into a handful of active atoms from a big dictionary. But there's growing evidence that transformers actually learn *factored representations* — different concepts occupy orthogonal subspaces, and within each subspace, the representations are **dense, not sparse** ([Shai et al., 2026](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.02385)). The sparsity assumption that the SAE edifice rests on may not hold.

So I went looking for a different framework. I found it in [*Principles and Practice of Deep Representation Learning*](https://ma-lab-berkeley.github.io/deep-representation-learning-book/index.html) from the Ma group at Berkeley.

---

## Why Not SAEs?

SAEs learn an **overcomplete dictionary** — 8× or 16× more atoms than dimensions — and decompose each activation into a sparse combination of them. MCR² asks a different question entirely. Instead of "which dictionary atoms are active?" it asks "how much representational volume does each concept occupy, and are the concept subspaces orthogonal?"

The decomposition is complete subspaces, not sparse features.

Both approaches require running images through the model to collect activations. The difference is what happens next. [TIDE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.07050) trains a separate SAE on each layer's activations — hours of GPU compute on 80K images per layer. SubspaceLens computes eigendecompositions of covariance matrices — seconds of CPU on 500 images. No learned parameters, no reconstruction loss to tune, no sparsity hyperparameter to sweep.

| | TIDE (SAE) | SubspaceLens (MCR²) |
|---|---|---|
| **Inference** | 80K images through PixArt | 500 images through PixArt |
| **Post-inference** | Train SAE per layer (hours) | Eigendecomposition per layer (seconds) |
| **Assumption** | 5% sparsity | Low-dim subspaces (can be dense) |
| **Model** | PixArt-α, 28 layers | PixArt-α, 28 layers |

## The Math

The book's argument: a deep network transforms data into a union of low-dimensional linear subspaces, one per concept. You measure how good this representation is with the **coding rate** — how many bits you need to encode a set of vectors up to precision $\varepsilon$:

$$R_\varepsilon(\mathbf{Z}) = \frac{1}{2} \log \det\left(\mathbf{I} + \frac{d}{N\varepsilon^2} \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{Z}^\top\right)$$

Compute it twice: once for everything together ($R_\varepsilon$), once per class averaged ($R_\varepsilon^c$). The difference is **rate reduction**:

$$\Delta R = R_\varepsilon(\mathbf{Z}) - R_\varepsilon^c(\mathbf{Z} \mid \Pi)$$

High $\Delta R$ = globally spread out, locally compact. That's what orthogonal subspaces look like.

The gradient of $\Delta R$ decomposes into two operators per layer:

- **Expansion** $\mathbf{E}^\ell$: pushes classes apart (increases $R_\varepsilon$)
- **Compression** $\mathbf{C}_k^\ell$: pulls each class tight (decreases $R_\varepsilon^c$)

Each transformer layer is one step of gradient ascent: $\mathbf{z}^{\ell+1} = \mathbf{z}^\ell + \eta(\mathbf{E}^\ell \mathbf{z}^\ell - \sum_k \mathbf{C}_k^\ell \mathbf{z}^\ell)$.

Here's where it clicked for me. The expansion operator $\mathbf{E}^\ell$ has a specific eigenstructure — it shrinks high-variance directions and preserves low-variance ones, via factors $1/(1 + \alpha\lambda_i)$. That's the same shrinkage the **Bayes optimal denoiser** uses. Not analogous. *Identical*. Denoising IS rate reduction.

So a diffusion transformer running denoising is simultaneously maximizing $\Delta R$. Each layer separates concepts into subspaces as a byproduct of removing noise. That's a testable prediction: $\Delta R$ should increase across layers.

To my knowledge, no one has applied MCR² coding rate metrics to diffusion transformer internals before. I tested the prediction.

---

## What I Measured

I took a frozen PixArt-α (28 transformer blocks, $d = 1152$) and hooked every block during denoising. No training, no SAEs. Just forward passes and eigenvalues.

**500 images**: 25 subjects × 20 seeds each. "A lion, detailed photograph" → 20 images from different random noise. "A cathedral, detailed photograph" → 20 more. Bare subjects, no adjectives or settings — so the only within-class variation comes from the model's stochasticity.

I measured with **two label schemes** on the same activations:
- **Coarse (5-class)**: animals vs. buildings vs. food vs. vehicles vs. landscapes
- **Fine (25-class)**: lion vs. hummingbird vs. octopus vs. cathedral vs. ...

Same $R_\varepsilon$. Different $R_\varepsilon^c$. This lets me ask: does the model just separate broad categories, or does it build per-subject subspaces?

I also hooked the unconditional branch (no text prompt, just null embeddings) to test whether the structure comes from text or from the model itself.

## What I Found

### $\Delta R$ increases 33× across layers

![Rate reduction across layers](./fig1_delta_r.png)
*$\Delta R$ from layer 0 to layer 27, with a consistent dip at layers 15–18.*

From ~30 at layer 0 to ~857 at layer 27. The dip at L15–18 is real and consistent across timesteps — those layers appear to mix subspaces for compositional reasoning before the final layers re-separate.

This isn't noise. A SIGReg normality test rejects the isotropic Gaussian null at 55–78% of random projections across every layer (expected: 1%).

### 22 dimensions out of 1152

![Effective rank across layers](./fig8_effective_rank.png)
*Effective rank peaks at ~69 around layer 10, then collapses to ~22 by layer 27.*

By the output layer, the model has compressed its representations into **~22 effective dimensions** — 98% of the 1152-dimensional space is unused. The model knows exactly how much room it needs.

### Something happens at layer 14

I ran an earlier sweep across 12 configurations (4 timesteps × 3 CFG scales) with more varied prompts — adjectives, settings, the works. (Those prompts used Cartesian product variation — adjectives × subjects × settings — which inflated within-class volume and masked per-subject compression. Bare-subject prompts isolate the signal. More on that below.) The pattern showed up everywhere:

![Rate landscape across all configs](./fig5_rate_landscape.png)
*$\Delta R$ across all 12 configs. The L14 peak and L15–18 dip are universal — they don't depend on timestep or guidance strength.*

And when I tracked subspace overlap across layers, layer 14 kept coming up as the point of maximum orthogonality — the moment where concept subspaces are most separated:

![Overlap trajectory across timesteps](./fig6_overlap_trajectory.png)
*Mean off-diagonal overlap drops to its minimum around L14, then rebounds. Earlier timesteps peak earlier (L11), later ones need more layers.*

Earlier denoising steps achieve peak separation sooner — the model needs fewer layers to separate coarse structure from noisy inputs, but nearly the full network to separate fine structure from near-clean inputs. Step 19 (almost done denoising) doesn't hit peak orthogonality until layer 25.

![5×5 overlap heatmap across layers](./fig7_overlap_heatmap.png)
*The 5×5 category overlap matrix at four layers. By L14, food and buildings barely share any variance. By L27, they've partially re-entangled.*

Whatever the model is doing at layer 14, it's the architectural sweet spot for concept separation. The layers after it sacrifice some orthogonality to prepare representations for the decoder — expanding globally rather than keeping subspaces clean.

It's a little like a UNet, actually. In a UNet you compress down to a bottleneck where everything is maximally abstract, then expand back out. Here, a flat-stack transformer with no skip connections is doing something similar: compress subspaces to peak orthogonality at L14, then re-expand for output. The architecture changed but the computational motif didn't.

### The model compresses per-subject, not just per-category

This is the key result. Same data, two ways of grouping:

| | L0 | L14 | L27 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Coarse** $R_\varepsilon^c / R_\varepsilon$ | 0.67 | 0.37 | 0.35 |
| **Fine** $R_\varepsilon^c / R_\varepsilon$ | 0.47 | 0.11 | **0.10** |

With fine labels, each subject's 20 representations occupy only **10%** of the total volume by layer 27. They start at 47%. The network compresses each subject from nearly half the total space to one-tenth. The model builds hierarchical structure: lions separate from cathedrals ($\Delta R = 857$ fine) *and* animals separate from buildings ($\Delta R = 616$ coarse). Fine-label $\Delta R$ is consistently 40% higher.

![Rate decomposition: coarse vs fine](./fig2_rate_decomposition.png)
*Same 500 images. Left: coarse labels mask the compression. Right: fine labels reveal it.*

$R_\varepsilon^c$ does still grow in absolute terms (26 → 91 nats) — the model preserves instance-level variation because it needs to denoise *this specific* lion, not classify lions generically. But proportionally it shrinks from 47% to 10%. This is the generative regime: absolute volumes grow, relative structure tightens.

### The structure is intrinsic

![Conditional vs unconditional rate reduction](./fig4_cond_uncond.png)
*Conditional (with text) and unconditional (null text) $\Delta R$ across layers. By L27, the difference is 0.1 nats.*

The model runs two forward passes per denoising step: one with the text prompt, one with nothing. I hooked both. By layer 27, conditional $\Delta R$ = 615.8 and unconditional = 615.7. The subspace geometry comes from the architecture and the denoising process, not the text.

### Activations are dense, not sparse

Hoyer sparsity ranges from 0.25 to 0.71 across layers. The most structured layers (L17–L21) are the *densest* (0.25–0.35) — the opposite of what a sparse dictionary assumes.

![25-subject overlap heatmap](./fig3_overlap_heatmap.png)
*Subspace overlap at layer 14. Block-diagonal = hierarchical nesting. Lions overlap more with elephants than with cathedrals.*

## What This Means

This isn't a proof that DiT representations are linear subspaces. The overlap values (0.23–0.30) tell me the separation is real but partial — real images live on nonlinear manifolds, and linear subspaces are an approximation. Mean-pooling erases spatial structure. I haven't steered anything. This is measurement, not intervention.

But. A metric from rate distortion theory — applied to a frozen model with no training, just eigenvalues — detects progressive, hierarchically nested, text-independent subspace structure in a diffusion transformer. The theory predicted it. The data confirmed it.

**What's next:** per-token analysis (spatial structure within images), other architectures (Flux, SANA), and the hard question — can you steer generation through subspace projection without falling off the activation manifold?

I started this because data visualization taught me that the most important thing you can do is show people what's hidden. SAEs gave me footholds into the model. MCR² might give me a map.

## Code

```python
def coding_rate(Z: Tensor, eps: float) -> Tensor:
    d, n = Z.shape[-2], Z.shape[-1]
    gram = Z.mT @ Z
    I = torch.eye(n, device=Z.device)
    _, logdet = torch.linalg.slogdet(I + (d / (n * eps**2)) * gram)
    return 0.5 * logdet
```

No GPU cluster, no SAE training, no feature labeling. Five lines of linear algebra.
